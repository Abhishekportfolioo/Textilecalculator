01 — INTRODUCTION

What Are Automotive Textiles?

▾
Definition: Automotive textiles (also called automotives or technical textiles for vehicles) refer to all fibre-based materials and structures used in the manufacture of automobiles, trucks, buses, and other motor vehicles.

A typical modern car contains 30–40 kg of textile materials, accounting for ~35% of a car's weight in textile components.

They serve functional roles: structural, safety, filtration, thermal/acoustic insulation, and aesthetic roles.

Automotive textiles are a subset of technical textiles — engineered fabrics designed for performance rather than aesthetics.

The field is driven by demands for weight reduction, durability, recyclability, safety, and comfort.

Why Textiles in Cars?

Weight Saving

Textile-based composites replace heavy metals, reducing vehicle weight and improving fuel efficiency.

Safety

High-tenacity fibres absorb crash energy in seatbelts, airbags, and tyre reinforcement.

Comfort

Upholstery and carpets provide thermal, acoustic, and tactile comfort inside the cabin.

Durability

Engineered fibres resist heat, abrasion, and chemicals in filters and engine compartments.

02 — FIBRES

Fibres Used in Automotive Applications

▾
Key Principle: Fibre selection is driven by the specific demands of each application — mechanical performance, heat resistance, moisture management, cost, and processability.

Natural Fibres

CottonWoolJuteSisalHempFlaxCoir

Cotton — soft, absorbent, comfortable; used in headliners, seat covers, and some woven fabrics. Low heat and abrasion resistance limits structural use.

Wool — excellent thermal and acoustic insulation, naturally flame-retardant, resilient; used in high-end upholstery and seat fabrics.

Jute / Sisal / Hemp / Flax — bast fibres; used as reinforcement in natural fibre composites (NFCs) for door panels, dashboards, parcel shelves. Lightweight, low cost, biodegradable.

Coir (coconut fibre) — used in seat cushions and floor mats; resilient and moisture-resistant.

ℹ️
Natural fibre composites (NFCs) in automotive interiors are gaining traction due to sustainability goals (e.g., EU End-of-Life Vehicle Directive mandates 85% recyclability).

Synthetic Fibres

Polyester (PET)Nylon (PA6/PA66)Polypropylene (PP)Polyacrylonitrile (PAN)Polyurethane (PU)PTFE

Polyester (PET) — most widely used synthetic in automotive textiles. High tensile strength, UV and abrasion resistance, low moisture absorption. Used in seat fabrics, carpets, seatbelts, airbags, tyre cords.

Nylon (PA6, PA66) — excellent tenacity, abrasion resistance, and fatigue resistance. Key fibre for airbag fabric and tyre cord. PA66 preferred over PA6 for higher melting point (265°C vs 220°C).

Polypropylene (PP) — lightweight, chemically resistant, low cost; widely used in carpets (needle-punched), filtration, and non-woven fabrics for headliners and trunk liners.

Polyacrylonitrile (Acrylic) — wool-like feel, UV resistant; used in convertible soft tops and some seat fabrics.

Polyurethane (PU) — used as foam (not a fibre per se), but also as elastane (spandex) fibres for stretch covers; laminated to seat fabrics.

PTFE (Teflon) — chemical and heat resistance; used in filtration membranes and seal tapes in engine areas.

High-Performance / Speciality Fibres

Aramid (Kevlar/Nomex)Carbon Fibre (PAN-based)Glass FibreBasalt FibreCeramic FibresUltra-HMPE (Dyneema)

Aramid (Para: Kevlar; Meta: Nomex) — para-aramid for tyre reinforcement and structural parts; meta-aramid for heat and flame protection. 5× stronger than steel by weight.

Carbon Fibre — extremely high stiffness-to-weight ratio; used in CFRP body panels, structural reinforcements, and racing car chassis. Expensive but growing in EVs for weight saving.

Glass Fibre (E-glass, S-glass) — used in GFRP composites for body parts, dashboards, headliners; good stiffness, low cost, heavy compared to carbon.

Ceramic Fibres (Alumina, Silica) — high-temperature insulation in exhaust systems and engine shields. Stable up to 1000–1600°C.

Man-Made Cellulosic Fibres

Viscose / RayonLyocell (Tencel)ModalCellulose Acetate

Viscose Rayon — used in high-modulus form (HM viscose) as tyre cord. Provides dimensional stability and adhesion to rubber. Good heat resistance versus nylon (less moisture sensitive).

Lyocell — eco-friendly solvent-spun cellulose. Emerging use in natural-fibre composites and non-woven fabrics for interiors.

03 — APPLICATION

Upholstery

▾
Definition: Automotive upholstery refers to seat coverings, headliners, door panels, dashboard covers, and armrests. It combines comfort, aesthetics, and durability.

Fibres Used

Polyester (PET) — dominant fibre; woven, knitted, or non-woven seat fabric; pill-resistant, UV-stable, easy to dye.

Nylon (PA) — abrasion-resistant; blended with PET for high-wear seat areas.

Wool — used in luxury/premium vehicles; natural comfort, flame retardancy, and temperature regulation.

Cotton — breathable and soft; often blended with PET for balance of comfort and durability.

Vinyl (PVC) / Leather substitutes — not fibres but layered textile backings; PU-coated fabrics used as leather alternatives.

Fabric Construction

Woven COMMON

Plain, twill, or jacquard weaves. High dimensional stability, defined pattern, good durability. Used on seat faces.

Knitted

Warp or weft knit. More stretch and conformability. Used on seat sides and bolsters where shaping is needed.

Non-woven

Needlepunched or spunbonded. Used for backing layers, headliners, boot liners.

Velour / Velvet

Cut-pile woven. Plush, premium feel. Used in luxury segment seating.

Manufacturing Process

01

Fibre Spinning / Yarn Formation

Polyester or nylon filaments produced by melt spinning; texturised to add bulk and softness.

02

Fabric Formation

Weaving, knitting, or non-woven production on appropriate machinery (rapier loom, warp knitting machine, needle loom).

03

Dyeing & Finishing

Piece dyeing or yarn dyeing; application of finishes (anti-soiling, anti-static, UV stabilisation, fire retardancy).

04

Lamination

Fabric laminated to PU foam (flame lamination or adhesive) for comfort and structure; often backed with scrim for dimensional stability.

05

Cutting & Sewing

Pattern pieces cut (CNC die cutting or laser cutting) and sewn into three-dimensional seat covers using industrial sewing machines.

⚠️
Upholstery fabrics must pass FMVSS 302 (USA) or ISO 3795 (EU) horizontal burn tests. Burn rate must not exceed 100 mm/min.

04 — APPLICATION

Carpets & Floor Coverings

▾
Function: Automotive carpets provide sound absorption, thermal insulation, moisture management, and aesthetic finishing of the vehicle floor, boot, and lower door panels.

Fibres Used

Nylon (PA6/PA66) — premium carpet fibre; excellent abrasion resistance, resilience, dyeability. Used in woven and tufted carpets.

Polypropylene (PP) — most commonly used due to low cost and good chemical resistance; used in needle-punched and tufted carpets; olefin fibre.

Polyester (PET) — recycled PET (rPET) growing in eco-cars; good colour retention and UV stability.

Carpet Types & Manufacture

Tufted MOST COMMON

Yarn loops (cut or uncut pile) inserted into a pre-woven primary backing using tufting needles. Secondary backing (latex or PU foam) bonded for stability. Fast, economical production. Used for most car floor carpets.

Needle-Punched

Fibres mechanically interlocked by barbed needles. No distinct pile. Used for boot liners, wheel arch liners, economical floor mats. Can be moulded into 3D shapes.

Woven

Wilton or Axminster weave. High-end, patterned. Used in luxury vehicles (e.g., Rolls Royce). Expensive; accurate pattern repeat, good durability.

Moulded / Preformed

Needle-punched or tufted carpets moulded under heat and pressure to exact floor contours. Laminated with EVA foam or heavy bitumen backing for sound damping.

Process (Needle-Punched Carpet)

01

Fibre Opening & Blending

PP or PET staple fibres opened, blended, and formed into a batt using carding or airlaying.

02

Needle Punching

Batt fed through needle loom; barbed needles entangle fibres vertically, creating a cohesive non-woven structure.

03

Backing Application

Foam, latex, bitumen, or thermoplastic backing applied by lamination for acoustic damping.

04

Moulding

Thermoforming under heat/pressure to replicate floor pan contours. Die cutting to final shape.

💡
Exam Tip: Needle-punched carpets are the most economical and most widely used type. Remember: they have NO distinct weave or pile — fibres are mechanically entangled by needles.

05 — APPLICATION

Preformed Parts (Interior Composites)

▾
Definition: Preformed parts are moulded textile-reinforced composite components used to form rigid or semi-rigid interior and exterior parts (door panels, headliners, parcel shelves, dashboards, trunk liners, A/B/C pillars).

Fibres / Reinforcements Used

Glass Fibre (E-glass) — chopped strand mat, woven roving, or continuous filament mat; used with polyester/epoxy resin in GFRP composites for body panels and structural parts.

Carbon Fibre — PAN-based; woven or UD prepregs; high stiffness; used in lightweight structural panels (doors, hoods) in premium/sports/EV cars.

Natural Fibres (Flax, Hemp, Kenaf, Jute, Sisal) — used as reinforcement in thermoplastic (PP) or thermoset matrices for door panels, dashboards, parcel shelves. Low density, bio-renewable, good acoustic damping.

Polyester / PP non-wovens — substrate and backing layers in interior trim; bonded with polyester binding fibres.

Headliner Construction

Structure

Multilayer sandwich: face fabric (PET/PP non-woven) → PU foam → substrate (glass fibre mat or thermoplastic honeycomb) → backing scrim.

Process

Layers assembled and moulded under heat/pressure. The substrate gives rigidity; PU foam provides acoustic insulation; face fabric gives the interior finish.

Natural Fibre Composite (NFC) Door Panel Manufacturing

01

Fibre Preparation

Natural fibres (e.g., hemp, kenaf) are decorticated, retted, and formed into mats by needlepunching or thermobonding with PP fibres.

02

Mat Layup

NFC mats stacked to required thickness and weight; often combined with a face layer of woven or knit decorative fabric.

03

Compression Moulding

Heated in an oven (PP melts ~165°C) then compression moulded in a cold steel tool; PP solidifies around natural fibres forming a stiff composite panel.

04

Trimming & Assembly

Die cut to final shape; clips and inserts attached; decorative surface film or fabric bonded if not already included.

ℹ️
Key advantage of NFC: 25–30% lighter than equivalent glass fibre composites, better acoustic performance, and end-of-life recyclability. Widely used by BMW, Mercedes, VW, Ford.

06 — APPLICATION

Tyres (Tyre Reinforcement)

▾
Role of Textiles: Textile cords (the "carcass" and "belt" layers) give the tyre its shape, dimensional stability, load-bearing capacity, and resistance to expansion. The rubber alone cannot do this — textile reinforcement is essential.

Tyre Structure (Textile Layers)

Tyre LayerLocationFibre UsedFunction
Carcass / Body PlyInner structural layerNylon (PA66), Polyester (PET), RayonPrimary load-bearing; maintains tyre shape under pressure
Belt / BreakerBetween carcass and treadSteel cord, Aramid (Kevlar), GlassCircumferential stability; prevents tread lift at speed
Cap Ply / OverlayOver beltsNylon (PA66), AramidHigh-speed performance; prevents belt edge lift
Bead ReinforcementBead areaSteel, Aramid, NylonAnchors tyre to rim; hoop strength
Flipper / ChipperBead wrapNylon, PolyesterProtects bead wire, adds lateral stiffness

Fibres Compared

Rayon (HM Viscose) — first tyre cord; high modulus, low moisture sensitivity vs regular rayon. Good thermal stability, strong adhesion to rubber via RFL dip. Still used in performance and run-flat tyres.

Nylon (PA66) — high strength, excellent fatigue resistance. Disadvantage: "flat-spotting" (temporary deformation at rest due to moisture absorption). Used in cap plies and bias tyres.

Polyester (PET) — dominant in radial car tyre carcasses. Low flat-spot tendency, high modulus, excellent adhesion with RFL (Resorcinol-Formaldehyde-Latex) dip. Most widely used tyre cord fibre today.

Aramid (Kevlar, para-type) — extremely high modulus and strength, very low elongation. Used in run-flat tyres (sidewall insert), belt reinforcement, and high-performance tyres. Expensive.

Glass Fibre — high modulus, good dimensional stability. Used in belts. Disadvantage: brittle under flexing fatigue.

Tyre Cord Manufacturing

01

Yarn Production

High-tenacity multi-filament yarns produced by melt spinning (PET, nylon) or dry-jet wet spinning (aramid). Very high draw ratios to maximise tenacity and modulus.

02

Twisting / Cabling

Two or three yarns plied and cabled (twisted in opposite directions) to form a balanced cord that resists compression buckling.

03

Dipping (RFL Treatment)

Cord passed through Resorcinol-Formaldehyde-Latex (RFL) bath and heat-cured. This creates adhesion between cord and rubber — without RFL, textiles do not bond to rubber.

04

Calendering

RFL-treated cords embedded in rubber sheets (skim coating) using calender rolls to form a rubberised ply.

05

Tyre Building & Curing

Plies assembled on a tyre-building drum; uncured tyre vulcanised in a mould under heat and pressure (150–180°C), forming the final tyre shape.

⚠️
Exam Tip: The RFL (Resorcinol-Formaldehyde-Latex) dip is the critical step that enables textile cords to bond to rubber. Always mention this in answers about tyre cord manufacture.

07 — APPLICATION

Safety Devices (Seatbelts & Airbags)

▾

A. Seatbelts

Function: Seatbelts restrain occupants during a crash. The webbing must absorb impact energy and withstand the peak loads without failing (~1500 kg force in a crash), while being flexible, comfortable, and durable in everyday use.

Fibre: Polyester (PET) — virtually universally used. High tenacity (>70 cN/tex), low elongation, UV resistance, no flat-spot issue, excellent retractor spring behaviour.

Construction: Woven Webbing — narrow-width warp-faced woven fabric (25–50 mm wide); produces high modulus, controlled elongation (8–12% at break), and a flat, smooth surface.

Weave: High-density plain or 2/2 twill — produces tight, firm structure with defined elongation characteristics for energy management.

Seatbelt Webbing Manufacturing

01

Yarn Selection

High-tenacity PET multifilament yarn (e.g., 1100 dtex, 1670 dtex); no twist or S-twist only; drawn to very high ratios for maximum tenacity.

02

Weaving (Narrow Fabric Loom)

Needle loom or rapier narrow-fabric machine produces webbing 25–75 mm wide at high speeds. The high warp density creates the required stiffness and strength.

03

Dyeing (Piece Dyeing)

Polyester dyed with disperse dyes at high temperature (130°C in pressurised jig or jet dyeing); fastness requirements are critical (lightfastness ≥ 5).

04

Finishing

Heat-setting to stabilise dimensions; stiffening or softening finishes applied for retractor performance; cutting to length.

ℹ️
Standards: ECE R16 (Europe) / FMVSS 209 (USA) specify minimum breaking strength (>14.7 kN), elongation, abrasion resistance, and light and heat fastness for seatbelt webbing.

B. Airbags

Function: Airbags inflate explosively (30–50 ms) to cushion occupants from impact. The fabric must withstand instantaneous deployment pressure, high temperatures from the inflator gas, and NOT burst or leak excessively, while being compact enough to fold into the steering wheel or dashboard.

Primary Fibre: Nylon (PA66) — chosen for high energy absorption, excellent fatigue resistance, toughness at low temperatures, and the ability to be woven at very fine counts for compactness.

Secondary / Cost Option: Polyester (PET) — lower moisture sensitivity than nylon (PA66 absorbs moisture, affecting folded storage performance). Increasingly used in newer designs.

Coated Airbag Fabric

  • Silicone or neoprene coating applied
  • Low gas permeability
  • Used for driver/passenger bags
  • Heat resistant coating
  • Stiffer — bulkier when folded

Uncoated (OPW) Airbag Fabric

  • One-Piece Woven technology
  • 3D woven seamlessly
  • Softer, more compact folding
  • Controlled gas permeability via weave
  • Used for side curtain/knee airbags

Airbag Fabric Manufacturing

01

Yarn Preparation

Fine, high-tenacity PA66 or PET multifilament yarns (e.g., 470 dtex, 700 dtex); low twist to maximise filament packing and strength.

02

Weaving (Plain Weave, OPW)

Tight plain-weave or OPW (One-Piece Woven) on jacquard-controlled rapier looms. OPW weaves inflatable chambers directly — no seams needed.

03

Scouring & Heat Setting

Fabric scoured to remove spin finishes; heat set to stabilise dimensions under deployment forces.

04

Coating (for coated type)

Silicone or chloroprene coating knife-coated or calendered onto one or both sides. Cured at 150–200°C.

05

Cutting & Sewing

Airbag panels cut to shape (if not OPW) and sewn into bags. Critical seam strength tested. Bags folded and packed into module.

⚠️
Key Distinction: OPW (One-Piece Woven) technology eliminates stitched seams, improving uniformity, reducing manufacturing steps, and allowing more complex shapes (side curtain airbags). Always compare coated vs uncoated in exam answers.

08 — APPLICATION

Filtration Textiles

▾
Types: Air filtration (engine intake, cabin HVAC), fuel filtration, oil filtration, transmission fluid filtration, and exhaust gas aftertreatment (DPF substrates).

Air Filters (Engine Intake)

Fibres: Polyester, Polypropylene, Glass Microfibre, Cellulose — formed into wet-laid or melt-blown non-woven media. Cellulose/synthetic blends used in standard paper-style filters.

Structure: Pleated non-woven — increased surface area. Gradient structure (coarse outer layer → fine inner layer) for depth filtration and dust-holding capacity.

Manufacture: Wet-laid paper process (cellulose/glass) or melt-blowing (PP) → pleating → end-caps applied (polyurethane potting) → housing.

Cabin Air Filters (HVAC / Pollen Filters)

Fibres: PET/PP non-woven + activated carbon layer + electret-charged melt-blown PP — multi-layer structure captures particles ≥ 0.3 μm, allergens, and odours.

Electret charging increases electrostatic attraction to particles without increasing resistance (ΔP), allowing high filtration efficiency at low pressure drop.

Oil & Fuel Filters

Fibres: Glass microfibre, polyester, nylon mesh (for fuel) — must resist prolonged contact with oils, fuels, and high temperatures (engine oil filter: up to 150°C).

Fuel filters: Fine nylon or PET mesh (micron rating 2–40 μm) prevents fuel injector blockage.

Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF) Substrates

Ceramic fibre (cordierite, silicon carbide) — not a woven textile but a ceramic fibre-based honeycomb structure. Captures soot particles and can withstand regeneration temperatures of 600–1000°C.

09 — APPLICATION

Engine Compartment Textiles

▾
Challenge: Engine compartment textiles are exposed to extreme conditions — temperatures 100–300°C (locally up to 600°C near exhausts), oils, fuels, coolants, and mechanical vibration. Only high-performance fibres survive.

Applications & Fibres

Engine Covers & Heat Shields INSULATION

Fibres: Ceramic fibres (alumina, silica), glass fibre, basalt fibre, and aluminium-foil-faced laminates. Withstand 500–1000°C.

Hose & Cable Sleeving

Fibres: Glass fibre braid, aramid braid, silicone-coated fibreglass. Protects hydraulic hoses, wiring looms, and fuel lines from heat and abrasion.

Gaskets & Seals

Fibres: Aramid (Kevlar/Nomex) fibres compressed with rubber/graphite binders. Replace asbestos (now banned); used in cylinder head gaskets, manifold gaskets.

Timing Belts

Fibres: Fibreglass cord or HSHT (High-Strength High-Temperature) PET cord embedded in HNBR (rubber). Must be dimensionally stable over engine life (~100,000 km).

V-Belts / Poly-V Belts

Fibres: Polyester or aramid tension cords in EPDM or neoprene matrix. Drive alternators, power steering pumps, A/C compressors.

Acoustic Engine Encapsulation

Fibres: Glass wool, polyester fibre pads, or melamine foam; moulded to underhood shape to reduce engine noise inside the cabin.

Exhaust Insulation Blankets

Fibres: High-silica glass or aluminosilicate ceramic fibre blankets; wrapped around catalytic converters and exhaust pipes to maintain operating temperature and reduce heat soak.

Wiring Harness Wrap

Fibres: Non-woven PET tape (abrasion wrap), glass fibre braid for high-temperature zones, or PTFE-coated glass for underhood wiring protection.

🚫
Historical Note: Asbestos was extensively used in automotive gaskets and insulation until the 1970s–1990s. It has been replaced by aramid fibre-based and glass-based alternatives following health and safety legislation.

Key Properties Required (Engine Zone)

Thermal Stability: Continuous use at 200–300°C; short-term at 500–1000°C (ceramic fibres).

Chemical Resistance: Engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, petrol/diesel, exhaust gases.

Vibration Resistance: Must survive 10+ years of engine vibration without fatigue failure (belts).

Dimensional Stability: Belts and timing gear must not stretch or creep over life of vehicle.

11 — EXAM PREP

Key Exam Facts & Must-Know Points

▾
💡
These are the high-frequency exam points. Each is a concise fact you must be able to state and explain.

🚗 A modern car contains 30–40 kg of textile materials, ~35% of total weight in textile-related components.

🏎️ Polyester (PET) is the MOST widely used fibre in automotive textiles overall — seatbelts, tyre cords, seat fabrics, carpets.

💺 Seatbelt webbing = high-tenacity PET woven on narrow fabric needle looms. Standards: ECE R16 / FMVSS 209.

🫧 Airbag fabric = Nylon PA66 (primary choice) plain-weave, either silicone-coated OR uncoated OPW (One-Piece Woven). OPW = no seams, used for curtain airbags.

🔄 RFL dip (Resorcinol-Formaldehyde-Latex) is the essential adhesion treatment applied to tyre cords to bond them to rubber. Without it, no adhesion.

🛞 Tyre cord fibre evolution: Cotton → Rayon → Nylon → Polyester (dominant today) → Aramid (high performance). PET replaced nylon because of lower flat-spot tendency.

🌿 Natural Fibre Composites (NFCs) use hemp/flax/jute + PP matrix, made by compression moulding. Used in door panels by BMW, Mercedes, VW. 25–30% lighter than GFRP.

🔥 Asbestos → Aramid: Asbestos was replaced in gaskets and engine insulation by aramid (Kevlar/Nomex) and glass fibres due to carcinogenicity.

⚙️ Needle-punched non-wovens = most economical carpet type (boot liners, floor mats); thermoformable for 3D shapes. No woven structure — mechanically entangled fibres.

♻️ EU ELV Directive requires 85% recyclability by weight → drives use of thermoplastic-matrix NFCs and monomaterial designs (e.g., all-PET components).

🌬️ Cabin air filters use electret-charged melt-blown PP to capture PM2.5 particles without high pressure drop. Key word: electret.

🧱 OPW (One-Piece Woven) for airbags = woven in one piece on a jacquard loom to form the 3D bag shape — eliminates seams, enables complex shapes like side curtain airbags.

🌡️ Ceramic fibres (aluminosilicate, silica) are used for exhaust/DPF insulation. They withstand 1000–1600°C. Not conventional textile fibres but fibre-based materials.

🎯 Flammability test for interior textiles = FMVSS 302 / ISO 3795 (horizontal burn, ≤ 100 mm/min). Know this standard!
