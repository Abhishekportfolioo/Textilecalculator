Overview of Composites

📌
Definition: A composite material is a combination of two or more constituent materials (a matrix and a reinforcement) with significantly different physical or chemical properties that, when combined, produce a material with characteristics different from the individual components.
🧱

Matrix (Binder)

  • Holds fibres together
  • Transfers load between fibres
  • Protects fibres from environment
  • Determines service temp & chemical resistance
🪡

Reinforcement (Fibre)

  • Carries principal mechanical loads
  • Provides stiffness and strength
  • Continuous, woven, or chopped forms
  • Oriented to match load paths
💡
Key Advantage: High specific strength (strength/weight) and specific stiffness (stiffness/weight) — superior to most metals for structural applications.

Types of Fibers

Types of Resins (Matrix)

Resins are categorised as Thermoset (cure irreversibly via chemical cross-linking) or Thermoplastic (melt and re-process).

🔥 Thermoset Resins

  • Epoxy: Most common; excellent adhesion; low shrinkage; 120–180 °C service
  • Polyester (UP): Low cost; used in marine/construction; higher shrinkage
  • Vinyl Ester: Better chemical resistance than polyester; improved toughness
  • Phenolic: Excellent fire/smoke/toxicity (FST); used in aircraft interiors
  • Bismaleimide (BMI): Higher temp than epoxy; 200–230 °C service
  • Polyimide (PMR-15, PETI): Extreme temp >300 °C; aerospace engines
  • Cyanate Ester: Low moisture uptake; good dielectric; radome structures

🔄 Thermoplastic Resins

  • PEEK (Polyether ether ketone): Excellent toughness; 250 °C; impact-tolerant
  • PPS (Polyphenylene sulfide): Chemical-resistant; 220 °C
  • PEI (Polyetherimide): FST-compliant; aircraft interiors
  • PA (Nylon 6/66): Low cost; moisture-absorbing; auto parts
  • Polypropylene: Cheapest; natural fibre composites
  • PEKK: Tougher than PEEK; additive manufacturing compatible
⚖️
Thermoset vs Thermoplastic: Thermosets offer better creep resistance and are easier to process at room temp. Thermoplastics offer unlimited shelf life, re-processability, weldability, and superior impact toughness — but require high-temperature/pressure processing.

04

Methods of Construction

1
Hand Lay-up / Wet Lay-up
Fibres placed manually on open mould; resin applied by brush/roller. Simple, low tooling cost. Suitable for large, low-volume parts (boats, wind blades). Low Cost Low Volume — high void content possible; operator-dependent quality.
2
Spray Lay-up
Chopped fibre and resin simultaneously sprayed onto mould. Faster than hand lay-up; lower fibre volume fraction; used for tubs, tanks, caravans. Random fibre orientation limits mechanical performance.
3
Autoclave / Prepreg Processing
Pre-impregnated fibre layers laid up; vacuum-bagged; cured in autoclave under elevated pressure (5–7 bar) and temperature (120–180 °C). Aerospace Grade — excellent fibre volume fraction (~60%), low voids (<1%). High capital cost.
4
Resin Transfer Moulding (RTM)
Dry fibre preform placed in closed mould; resin injected under pressure. Good surface finish both sides; controlled fibre volume. Variants: Light RTM, HP-RTM (fast automotive), VARI (vacuum-assisted). Medium-High Volume
5
Vacuum Infusion / VARTM
Dry fibre on one-sided mould; vacuum draws resin through; cured at room temperature. Low tooling cost; large parts possible (yacht hulls, wind blades). Cannot achieve as high Vf as autoclave.
6
Filament Winding
Continuous fibre tow wound over rotating mandrel in precise helical/hoop patterns. Optimal for pressure vessels, pipes, rocket motor casings. High Vf — limited to convex shapes.
7
Pultrusion
Continuous fibres pulled through resin bath, shaped in heated die — constant cross-section profiles (I-beams, channels, rods). Continuous Production — very high throughput, excellent fibre alignment along pull axis.
8
Sheet Moulding Compound (SMC) / Compression Moulding
Glass/carbon mat pre-impregnated; heated in matched metal dies at 150–170 °C. High-volume automotive parts; good surface finish. Short fibres → lower properties than continuous.
9
Automated Fibre Placement (AFP) / Automated Tape Laying (ATL)
Robotic head places narrow prepreg tapes/slit tows with precise angle control. Used for large aerospace structures (fuselage skins, wing covers). Reduces labour; enables complex tow-steering; reduces scrap.
10
Braiding (2D / 3D)
Fibres interlaced at ±θ angles over mandrel or through-thickness. Near-net-shape preforms; excellent damage tolerance and torsional stiffness. Covered in detail in later sections.

Types of Preforms

📋
Preform: A shaped arrangement of fibres prior to resin infusion or consolidation. It defines the fibre architecture, orientation, and near-net shape of the final part.

1. Unidirectional (UD) Tape / Tow

All fibres aligned in one direction. Highest mechanical properties along fibre axis. Used in laminates stacked at multiple angles. Forms: prepreg tape, dry UD fabric with stitch binder.

2. Woven Fabrics

Plain weave: 1×1 interlace — high crimp, moderate strength, good handleability
Twill weave: Diagonal pattern (2×2, 3×3); lower crimp; better drape — aerospace panels
Satin weave: Minimal interlace (4H, 5H, 8H); lowest crimp; excellent surface finish; poor stability
Harness satin: Used for complex-curved surfaces

3. Non-Crimp Fabrics (NCF)

Multiple UD plies stitched together (0°, ±45°, 90°). No weave crimp → superior in-plane properties vs woven. Available as biaxial, triaxial, quadraxial. Used with RTM/VARTM. High Performance

4. Chopped Strand Mat (CSM)

Short randomly-oriented glass fibres bound with powder or emulsion. Quasi-isotropic but low Vf (20–25%). Used in hand lay-up/spray-up. Cheap; good conformability.

5. Braided Preforms (2D & 3D)

Fibres interlaced at bias angles. 2D braid: tubular or flat; 3D braid: through-thickness fibres — addressed in detail in Section 8 & 9.

6. Knitted Fabrics

Fibre loops interlocked — very high conformability; can drape complex 3D surfaces. Low in-plane properties due to high fibre curvature. Warp-knit NCF is distinct (no loops in structural plies).

7. Sandwich Core (Preform Concept)

Face skins + lightweight core (Nomex honeycomb, foam, balsa). High bending stiffness-to-weight. Cores: PVC foam, PET foam, PMI foam, aluminium honeycomb, Nomex honeycomb.

8. Z-Pinned / Stitched Preforms

Through-thickness reinforcement in 2D laminate via stitching (Kevlar/carbon thread) or metallic Z-pins. Dramatically improves delamination resistance and Mode I/II fracture toughness.

06

Preform Properties

Fibre Volume Fraction (Vf)
Ratio of fibre volume to total composite volume. Higher Vf → better mechanical properties. Typical: UD/woven 55–65%, NCF 55–60%, CSM 20–30%.
Areal Weight (gsm)
Mass per unit area (g/m²). Controls ply thickness and affects permeability in LCM processes.
Drapeability
Ability to conform to curved tooling without wrinkling. Woven > NCF > UD. Critical for complex double-curvature parts.
Permeability
Ease of resin flow through preform (important for RTM/VARTM). Braided/woven higher permeability than tight UD; binder can reduce permeability.
Nesting / Compaction
Ability of plies to compact under pressure — affects achievable Vf and spring-back.
Binder Content
Thermoplastic binder (1–5%) holds preform shape for handling. Can affect resin flow if excessive.
Architecture
Fibre crimp angle, interlace frequency, and orientation determine stiffness, strength, and damage tolerance.
Damage Tolerance
Through-thickness reinforcement (3D, stitched, Z-pins) dramatically improves delamination resistance (Mode I GIc, Mode II GIIc).
Near-Net-Shape
Preforms closer to final part shape reduce trim waste and handling. Key benefit of braiding and 3D weaving.

✅ UD Tape Strengths

  • Highest specific strength & stiffness
  • Precise fibre placement
  • Low crimp → better translation efficiency

❌ UD Tape Limitations

  • Poor drape on complex curves
  • Sensitive to delamination
  • Many ply angles needed for quasi-isotropic

✅ Woven Fabric Strengths

  • Excellent drape & conformability
  • Balanced in-plane properties
  • Easy handling & draping

❌ Woven Fabric Limitations

  • Fibre crimp reduces axial properties (~10–15%)
  • Complex crimped geometry
  • Limited through-thickness strength

3-Dimensional (3D) Fabrics

🔬
Motivation: Conventional 2D laminates have negligible through-thickness (z-direction) reinforcement → vulnerable to interlaminar delamination under impact or out-of-plane loads. 3D fabrics integrate z-direction fibres to solve this.
  • Architecture Types: 3D orthogonal (x, y, z yarns no interlacing), layer-to-layer interlock, through-thickness angle interlock, and warp-weave architectures.
  • 3D Orthogonal: Warp (x), weft (y), and binder/z-yarn independent — highest in-plane properties preserved; binder yarn holds layers.
  • Layer-to-Layer Interlock: Warp yarn links adjacent layers — moderate z-reinforcement with good in-plane properties.
  • Through-Thickness Angle Interlock: Warp traverses ALL layers — maximum z-reinforcement; some in-plane property sacrifice.
  • Properties: High delamination resistance (GIc ×5–10 vs 2D laminate); excellent damage tolerance under impact; no catastrophic delamination propagation.
  • Limitations: Complex loom equipment; fibre damage during weaving; lower in-plane properties than UD for same fibre content.
  • Applications: Fan blades (GE LEAP engine), T-joints, I-beams, thick-section aerospace structures.
  • Concept: Carriers on 2D track plate travel in competing paths — fibres interlace through entire thickness of the preform.
  • Architectures: 4-step (1×1) braid — Cartesian; 2-step braid — axial yarns dominant with bias carriers; Circular 3D braid.
  • Braid angle range: 10° – 75° from preform axis; controls axial vs. torsional stiffness trade-off.
  • Advantages: Seamless net-shape preforms, extremely high damage tolerance, no interlaminar planes, complex cross-sections (I, C, T, hollow).
  • Disadvantages: Limited to relatively small cross-sections; slow production; complex yarn paths complicate analysis.
  • 2D fabric layers joined by through-thickness stitching (lock stitch, modified lock stitch, chain stitch).
  • Materials: Kevlar, carbon, glass thread; 0.2–0.5 mm pitch; 1–4% volume fraction z-yarn.
  • Benefits: Retrofit onto existing 2D fabrics; large area coverage; automated stitching possible (Robbin Needle System).
  • Drawback: In-plane fibre damage from needle penetration; reduced in-plane strength.
  • Metallic (titanium, steel) or CFRP pins (0.28–0.5 mm dia.) inserted through-thickness into uncured prepreg laminates using ultrasonic insertion tool (UAZ).
  • Areal density: 0.5–4%; volume fraction: 0.5–2%
  • Properties: Mode I GIc ×6–10 increase; Mode II ×3–5; improved pull-off strength at joints and flanges.
  • Applications: T-joints, co-cured stiffeners, fuselage frame attachments, trailing edge wedges.
  • Drawback: Compressive knockdown ~10–20% due to fibre waviness from pin insertion.

Triaxially Braided Materials

🎯
Definition: Triaxial braid incorporates three yarn families: bias yarns at +θ and −θ (braid yarns) PLUS axial yarns (0°) locked between them. Unlike biaxial braid (2 directions only), the axial tows dramatically improve longitudinal stiffness and strength.

Architecture Details

  • Bias angle (θ): Typically ±30°, ±45°, or ±60° — adjustable during braiding
  • Axial tow placement: Between bias carriers — can be different fibre type (e.g., carbon bias + glass axial)
  • Cover factor: Proportion of surface covered by braided tows — affects resin content
  • Mandrel shapes: Circular, rectangular, T, I, C, hat sections — near net shape
  • Hybrid possible: Carbon bias ± Kevlar axial for multi-functional

Mechanical Advantages

  • Balanced biaxial and axial properties
  • Excellent torsional and axial stiffness combination
  • Damage tolerance — no delamination planes
  • Energy absorption in crash: progressive crushing
  • Notch-insensitivity (interlocked fibres blunt crack)
  • Near-net shape → less waste

  • Maypole braider: Carriers on sinusoidal track; alternate carriers move in opposite directions creating ±θ braid yarns.
  • Axial yarn insertion: Separate spools supply 0° yarns stationary through the fell point alongside bias carriers.
  • Fell point: Point where yarns converge onto mandrel — controls braid angle (take-up speed vs. carrier speed ratio).
  • Overbraiding: Multiple layers applied sequentially to build up wall thickness.
  • RTM/VARTM after braiding: Preform removed from mandrel (if collapsible) or infused on mandrel.
  • Automated braiding: Robotic arm moves mandrel through stationary braider — enables variable angle and complex shapes.
  • Aircraft fuselage frames: Airbus A380 fuselage frames use 3D braided carbon CFRP — first large-scale structural use.
  • Fan blades (aeroengine): GE GE90 fan blade; CFM LEAP fan blade — 3D woven carbon; triaxial provides bird-strike tolerance and vibration damping.
  • Automotive crash structures: BMW i-series, Lamborghini monocoque — braided CFRP tubes for progressive crushing energy absorption.
  • Bicycle frames: Overbraided CFRP tubes joined with RTM nodes.
  • Medical prosthetics and stents.
  • Wind turbine blade roots: Circular triaxial braid provides quasi-isotropic bolt-load-bearing region.
  • Helicopter drive shafts and rotor torque tubes.
  • Pressure vessels and rocket casings.
  • Classical Laminate Theory (CLT): Treat as equivalent laminate [+θ / 0 / −θ] — quick but ignores crimp and interlacing.
  • Fabric Geometry Model (FGM): Accounts for yarn path geometry and undulation.
  • Mosaic Model: RUC divided into blocks of UD material; rule of mixtures at scale.
  • Finite Element Unit Cell: Detailed periodic FE model of repeating unit cell — most accurate; computationally expensive.
  • Meso-scale FE: Commercial tools (WiseTex, TexGen, ABAQUS plugin) model tow paths explicitly.
