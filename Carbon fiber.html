Introduction & Overview

What are Carbon Fibres?

Carbon fibres are fibres containing at least 92% carbon by weight, obtained by the controlled pyrolysis (thermal decomposition in inert atmosphere) of organic precursor fibres. They are not drawn from molten carbon — they are derived from organic polymer precursors.

Key Fact: Carbon fibres with >99% carbon content and a highly ordered graphite-like structure are called graphite fibres.
STEP 1 Precursor Fibre Rayon / PAN / Pitch
200–400 °C Preoxidation Stabilization
1000–1500 °C Carbonization Inert atmosphere
>2000 °C Graphitization Optional
Historical Note: Thomas Edison used carbon fibres (from pyrolyzed cotton thread) as filaments in his early light bulbs (1879). Modern high-performance carbon fibres were developed by Roger Bacon (1958) and refined at Royal Aircraft Establishment, UK (1963).

03

Viscose Rayon Route

Viscose Rayon as Precursor

Viscose rayon is a cellulose-based fibre (regenerated cellulose). Its empirical formula is (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ. It was the first precursor used commercially for making carbon fibres.

Characteristics of Rayon Precursor

  • Source: Wood pulp (cellulose) processed via viscose process
  • Carbon content: ~44% in the precursor fibre
  • Structure: Semi-crystalline polymer with β-1,4-glycosidic bonds
  • Drawback: Very low carbon yield (~25–30%) due to high oxygen content

Processing Steps for Rayon-Based CF

Step 1: Drying & Degumming (RT to 150°C) +
Moisture removed; minor weight loss; no chemical change. Endothermic stage.
Step 2: Dehydration (150–240°C) — Preoxidation +
Loss of H₂O from cellulose; C=C double bonds begin to form. The glycosidic bonds break. The exothermic decomposition of cellulose begins, producing H₂O, CO, CO₂, and various volatile organics. A Lewis acid catalyst (e.g. ZnCl₂, NH₄H₂PO₄) is often used to improve yield.
Step 3: Carbonization (400–1500°C) +
Aromatic ring condensation; elimination of remaining H, O, N atoms. Turbostratic carbon structure forms. Significant mass loss continues.
Step 4: Graphitization (>2000°C, optional) +
Hot stretching applied to improve crystallite alignment and increase modulus. Without stretching, fibres have low modulus (25–50 GPa).
C₆H₁₀O₅ → 6C + 5H₂O (simplified dehydration)
Important: Rayon-derived CF has lower modulus and strength than PAN-derived CF. They are used mainly in ablative heat shields (spacecraft re-entry) and insulation due to their excellent thermal properties.
04

PAN Route (Dominant Route)

Polyacrylonitrile (PAN) as Precursor

PAN is an addition polymer of acrylonitrile (CH₂=CHCN). It is the most widely used precursor (~90% of commercial CF production). The pendant nitrile (-CN) groups are key to the stabilization chemistry.

(-CH₂-CH(CN)-)ₙ — Polyacrylonitrile

Precursor Preparation

  • PAN copolymers used (typically 93–95% AN with 5–7% comonomers like methacrylic acid, itaconic acid)
  • Comonomers lower cyclization temperature and improve processing
  • Wet-spun or dry-jet wet-spun into fibres; then drawn to align polymer chains (imparts preferred orientation)

Stage 1 — Preoxidation / Stabilization (200–300°C in Air)

Most critical step for PAN. The fibre is heated under tension in air. Three main reactions occur:

  • Cyclization: Adjacent –CN groups undergo intramolecular cyclization forming a ladder polymer (pyridine ring structure). Exothermic.
  • Dehydrogenation: H₂ is removed, C=C double bonds form within and between chains → conjugated system
  • Oxidation: O₂ incorporation → forms –OH, C=O groups → cross-linking between chains → thermally stable structure
–(CH₂–CH–CN)– → Cyclic ladder polymer (dark brown/black fibre)
Why tension? Maintained under tension to prevent fibre shrinkage (PAN shrinks ~30% if unconstrained). Tension preserves molecular orientation → better mechanical properties in final fibre.

Chemical Changes

  • Colour changes: white → yellow → brown → black
  • Density increases from ~1.17 to ~1.35 g/cm³
  • The fibre becomes infusible (non-melting) — essential before high-temperature treatment
  • C:N ratio changes due to cyclization and dehydrogenation

Stage 2 — Carbonization (1000–1500°C in N₂/Ar)

  • Non-carbon atoms (H, O, N) eliminated as HCN, H₂O, N₂, CO, CO₂
  • Aromatic ring condensation → turbostratic graphite-like layers
  • Carbon content rises from ~60% (stabilized) to >92%
  • Crystallite size (La, Lc) and crystallinity increase with temperature
  • Fibre loses ~50% of its initial mass but retains shape (pseudomorphism)
  • Small crystallites, many defects → high strength

Stage 3 — Graphitization (2000–3000°C, optional)

  • Applied to produce high-modulus (Type I) fibres
  • Further ordering → larger graphite crystallites, reduced layer spacing (d₀₀₂ → 0.3354 nm for perfect graphite)
  • Crystallite alignment along fibre axis increases → higher modulus but lower strength
  • Electrical and thermal conductivity increase dramatically
05

Pitch Route

Pitch as Precursor

Pitch is a complex mixture of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). Two types are used:

  • Isotropic pitch (coal tar or petroleum pitch) — random molecular arrangement → lower quality CF
  • Mesophase pitch (anisotropic) — partially ordered liquid crystalline state → high modulus CF

Isotropic Pitch Route

  • Low cost; low-performance general purpose CF
  • Softening point ~80–120°C; melt-spun into fibres
  • Preoxidized at 200–300°C to infusibilize → carbonized → CF
  • Properties: Modulus 30–60 GPa, Tensile Strength ~600 MPa

Mesophase Pitch Route (High Performance)

Mesophase pitch is produced by:

  1. Thermal treatment of isotropic pitch at 400–450°C for extended periods
  2. PAH molecules form disc-like structures that stack into liquid crystal (nematic) domains
  3. The mesophase content increases with time/temperature
  4. 100% mesophase pitch is used for best properties
Melt Spinning: Mesophase pitch is melt-spun through a spinneret. The liquid crystal molecules align along the fibre axis due to shear and extensional flow → this pre-alignment is key to the high modulus of the final fibre.

Processing Advantage

  • High carbon yield (~80–90%) — economical despite higher precursor cost
  • Near-perfect graphitizable structure → highest modulus (~900 GPa possible)
  • Final fibres: High modulus, good thermal/electrical conductivity
🧠 Pitch vs PAN: Key Distinction

PAN → High Strength (many defects = crack arrest) | Pitch → High Modulus (near-perfect alignment = stiff)
Think: PAN = Performance in strength; Pitch = Perfectly stiff

Preoxidation (Stabilization)

Purpose of Preoxidation

To convert the thermoplastic precursor into a thermosetting (infusible, non-combustible) fibre that can survive subsequent high-temperature treatments without melting, fusing, or degrading.

Without preoxidation: The precursor fibre would simply melt or burn during carbonization, destroying the fibrous morphology.

Physical & Chemical Changes During Preoxidation

DensityIncreases (~1.17 → ~1.35 g/cm³ for PAN)Cross-linking, oxygen uptake

Preoxidation of PAN — Reaction Details

  • Cyclization: –CN + –CN → six-membered pyridine rings (ladder polymer)
  • Dehydrogenation: –CH₂–CH– → –CH=C– (removes H, forms C=C bonds)
  • Oxidation: –CH= + O₂ → –C(=O)–, –OH; provides cross-links
Key: The rate must be controlled — too fast causes runaway exotherm and thermal damage; too slow is uneconomical. Typical residence time: 1–2 hours at 200–300°C.
07

Carbonization

Process Parameters

  • Temperature range: 1000–1500°C (for Type II / high-strength CF)
  • Atmosphere: Inert — N₂ or Ar (prevents oxidation)
  • Tension: Maintained to preserve orientation
  • Heating rate: Slow rate below 600°C (volatile evolution); faster above

Chemical Changes During Carbonization

  1. 300–600°C: Elimination of N as HCN, NH₃; H₂ evolution; further aromatic condensation begins
  2. 600–1000°C: Rapid elimination of residual H, N, O as gaseous by-products; graphene layer formation; carbon content rises to >95%
  3. 1000–1500°C: Turbostratic carbon fully established; layer spacing d₀₀₂ decreases (from ~0.365 nm to ~0.345 nm); defect density still high
By-products: HCN, N₂, H₂O, CO, CO₂, H₂ (monitored for safety)

Physical Changes During Carbonization

PropertyChange

DiameterDecreases (5–8 μm from ~12 μm PAN fibre)
DensityIncreases to ~1.7–1.8 g/cm³
Tensile strengthRises sharply above 600°C; peaks at ~1300°C for Type II

08

Graphitization

Process Parameters

  • Temperature: 1500–3000°C (typically 2500–3000°C for high-modulus)
  • Atmosphere: Inert (Ar preferred over N₂ at these temperatures)
  • Time: Seconds to a few minutes (very short at highest temperatures)
  • Optional stretch: ~5–10% elongation at temperature to improve crystallite alignment

Structural Changes During Graphitization

  • Turbostratic carbon (random layer stacking) → 3D ordered graphite structure (A-B-A-B stacking)
  • d₀₀₂ decreases: ~0.345 nm (carbonized) → 0.3354 nm (perfect graphite)
  • Crystallite dimensions La (crystallite width) and Lc (crystallite height) increase
  • Preferred orientation of basal planes along fibre axis improves (OA, orientation angle decreases)
  • Carbon content approaches 99%+
  • Sp² hybridized carbon network maximized
Trade-off: Graphitization increases modulus but reduces tensile strength (fewer defects = fewer crack arrest points; crack propagates more easily along graphene planes).

Structure of Carbon Fibre

Atomic-Level Structure

Carbon fibres consist of turbostratic graphite — stacked graphene layers (hexagonal carbon network) with:

  • Strong covalent sp² C–C bonds within layers (very short, ~0.142 nm)
  • Weak van der Waals forces between layers
  • Layer spacing (~0.34–0.365 nm) — larger than perfect graphite (0.3354 nm) due to disorder
  • Turbostratic: layers are roughly parallel but randomly rotated about the c-axis (no A-B-A-B stacking)

Microstructure — The "Skin-Core" Model

  • Skin region: Graphene planes aligned more parallel to fibre axis → higher orientation → better tensile properties
  • Core region: More disordered, wrinkled graphene layers, defects, voids
  • Diameter: typically 5–10 μm
  • Cross-section shape: circular (PAN) or kidney/lobular (some PAN grades), circular/irregular (pitch)
Crystallite Parameters (from XRD):
Lc = crystallite height (c-direction), La = crystallite width (a-direction),
d₀₀₂ = interlayer spacing, OA = orientation angle (Herman's orientation factor)

Properties of Carbon Fibres

Mechanical Properties

Tensile Strength 2–7 GPa
Young's Modulus 200–900 GPa
Density 1.75–1.95 g/cm³
Elongation at Break 0.3–2.4%
Specific Strength >2 GPa·cm³/g
Compressive Str. ~60–80% of tensile
Specific modulus (modulus/density) of CF is 3–5× that of steel — this is why CF composites replace metals in aerospace.

Thermal Properties

  • Axial CTE: Slightly negative (−0.1 to −0.5 × 10⁻⁶/K) — unique; fibre contracts on heating in the axial direction
  • Radial CTE: Positive, ~7–10 × 10⁻⁶/K
  • Axial thermal conductivity: 10–1000 W/m·K (high-modulus pitch: up to 1000 W/m·K — higher than copper!)
  • Melting point: No true melting — sublimes above 3600°C
  • Oxidation onset: ~400–450°C in air; much higher in inert atmospheres

Electrical Properties

  • CF is electrically conductive (unlike glass or aramid fibres)
  • Resistivity: ~1.5–2.5 × 10⁻⁵ Ω·m (Type II) to ~9 × 10⁻⁶ Ω·m (Type I)
  • Can cause galvanic corrosion when in contact with metals → requires isolation
  • Used for electromagnetic shielding (EMI)

Chemical & Surface Properties

  • Chemically inert to most solvents, acids (except strong oxidizing acids), and bases
  • High surface area; surface treatment (oxidation, sizing) needed for good fibre–matrix adhesion in composites
  • Surface treatment methods: Anodic oxidation (electrochemical), gas-phase oxidation, wet chemical oxidation → introduce –COOH, –OH, C=O groups
  • Sizing: Thin polymer coating applied to protect fibre and improve wettability with resin

Applications of Carbon Fibres

CF is almost always used as a reinforcement in composite materials (CFRP — Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer/Plastics), typically with epoxy, polyimide, or thermoplastic matrices.

Aerospace & Defence

High Priority Largest Segment
  • Aircraft structural components: fuselage skins, wing spars, empennage (Boeing 787: ~50% CFRP by weight)
  • Rocket motor casings, satellite structures, solar panels
  • Military aircraft (F-22, F-35): airframe components for stealth and lightweight performance
  • Ablative heat shields on spacecraft re-entry vehicles (rayon-based CF used here)

Automotive

Growing Sector
  • Racing cars (Formula 1): entire chassis, monocoque, bodywork
  • High-end production vehicles (BMW i3/i8, Lamborghini, Ferrari): body panels, roof structures
  • Drive shafts, brake discs (C/C composites), springs
  • Electric vehicle battery enclosures and structural frames

Sports & Leisure

Consumer
  • Tennis racquets, golf club shafts, fishing rods
  • Bicycle frames, helmets, rowing shells
  • Hockey sticks, archery bows, ski poles
  • Prosthetic limbs and orthopaedic devices

Industrial & Civil Engineering

  • Wind turbine blades (CF/glass hybrid)
  • Pressure vessels and pipelines (oil & gas)
  • Structural reinforcement of bridges and buildings (CFRP wrapping, plates)
  • Chemical plant equipment resistant to corrosion
  • Flywheels for energy storage

Electrical & Thermal Applications

  • EMI shielding: Electronics enclosures, aircraft avionics
  • Heating elements: Radiant heaters, floor heating
  • Electrodes: Fuel cells, capacitors, lithium-ion battery electrodes
  • Carbon-carbon (C/C) composites: Brake discs for aircraft and racing cars, rocket nozzles
  • Thermal management: High-modulus pitch CF with thermal conductivity >500 W/m·K used in electronic cooling

Medical & Biomedical

  • X-ray tables and CT scanner components (radiolucent CF allows unobstructed imaging)
  • Prosthetic limbs: running blades (e.g. Flex-Foot), arm prostheses
  • Surgical instruments, endoscopes
  • Carbon fibre scaffolds for tissue engineering (biocompatibility demonstrated)
Special Application — Carbon-Carbon (C/C) Composites: Made by infiltrating CF preforms with carbon matrix (chemical vapour infiltration or resin impregnation + pyrolysis). Retain mechanical properties above 2000°C in inert atmospheres. Used in aircraft brakes, rocket nozzles, and re-entry heat shields.

Quick Revision Quiz

Test your understanding — click an option to check your answer.

1. Which precursor gives the highest carbon yield during pyrolysis?

✅ Correct! Mesophase pitch has the highest carbon yield (~80–90%) because it is already highly aromatic with minimal heteroatoms to lose.

2. The purpose of maintaining tension during PAN preoxidation is to:

✅ Correct! PAN shrinks ~30% during preoxidation. Tension preserves chain alignment, directly impacting final fibre mechanical properties.

3. Turbostratic carbon differs from graphite in that turbostratic carbon has:

✅ Correct! Turbostratic carbon has parallel but randomly rotated graphene layers → larger d₀₀₂ (~0.34–0.365 nm) than perfect graphite (0.3354 nm).

4. Graphitization increases modulus but decreases tensile strength because:

✅ Correct! A more perfect crystal has fewer defects. In strength theory, defects (and misaligned crystallites) act as crack-arrest barriers. Remove them and strength drops.

5. Which key reaction occurs during PAN preoxidation that makes the fibre infusible?

✅ Correct! The cyclization creates a ladder polymer; oxidative cross-linking connects chains in 3D → no longer thermoplastic. This is the essence of PAN stabilization.
