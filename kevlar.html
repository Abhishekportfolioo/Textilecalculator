Understanding Kevlar

Manufacture, Structure, Properties & Applications — complete exam notes compiled from lecture slides.

PPTA Polymer Liquid Crystal Spinning Aromatic Polyamide Para-linkage Du Pont, 1965
01

Overview & Discovery

Kevlar is a para-aramid synthetic fibre developed by Stephanie Kwolek at Du Pont in 1965. It is the commercial trade name for poly(p-phenylene terephthalamide) — PPTA. The key breakthrough was the discovery that para-linkage (connecting benzene rings at 1 and 4 positions) gives chains a rod-like, extended conformation that allows them to crystallise into highly ordered structures, producing exceptionally strong fibres.

💡
Exam Key Point: Kevlar belongs to the class of lyotropic liquid-crystal polymers. The polymer forms an ordered, anisotropic solution in concentrated sulphuric acid, and this pre-ordered state is directly "transferred" into the fibre during spinning — this is why no post-draw step is needed (it is spin-oriented, not spin-drawn).
02

Nomex vs Kevlar — Structural Difference

Both Nomex and Kevlar are aromatic polyamides but differ crucially in how the benzene rings are connected into the backbone:

Nomex (meta-aramid)

  • Benzene rings linked through 1 and 3 positions (meta-linkage)
  • Chain adopts a kinked / bent conformation
  • Less crystallisable — chains cannot pack efficiently
  • Compact packing is disallowed
  • Lower strength → mainly used for heat-resistant applications

Kevlar (para-aramid)

  • Benzene rings linked through 1 and 4 positions (para-linkage)
  • Chain adopts a rod-like, fully extended conformation
  • More crystallisable — chains pack tightly and efficiently
  • Compact packing is allowed
  • Very high tensile strength and modulus
🔑
The motivation for discovering Kevlar was the poor crystallisability and thus inferior mechanical performance of Nomex. Switching from meta- to para-linkage was the structural solution that unlocked high-performance behaviour.
03

Polycondensation Synthesis

Kevlar (PPTA) is synthesised by low-temperature polycondensation of two monomers:

Reactants H₂N—⬡—NH₂  +  ClOC—⬡—COCl p-Phenylene Diamine (PPD)        Terephthaloyl Chloride (TCl) Solvent: n-methyl pyrrolidone (NMP)  |  Temp: 10 – 20°C ↓ [ –HN—⬡—NH–OC—⬡–CO– ]ₙ  +  2HCl p-Phenylene Terephthalamide (PPTA)

Polycondensation Conditions

  • Temperature: 10–20°C — kept low to avoid polymer degradation and minimise side reactions
  • Reaction mixture is vigorously stirred to ensure homogeneity
  • Products are ground in water, filtered, and the resulting polymer is thoroughly washed to remove HCl by-product and solvent
  • Polymer with minimum viscosity (4) in H₂SO₄ is preferred — at this molecular weight, maximum anisotropy is achieved in the spinning dope
⚠️
Why low temperature? The amide bond can hydrolyse or the aromatic amine can undergo side reactions at elevated temperatures, reducing molecular weight and degrading the final fibre properties.
04

Dope Preparation & Characteristics

The dry PPTA polymer must be dissolved into a spinning dope before fibre formation. The dope is a concentrated solution of PPTA in 100% H₂SO₄ (water-free sulphuric acid).

Spinning Solution Composition

  • 10–20 wt% polymer in 100% H₂SO₄
  • Temperature: 100°C for normal fibre; 70–90°C for high-tenacity fibre
  • Anhydrous acid is essential — water would cause premature precipitation

Liquid Crystal Behaviour of the Dope

The dope passes through distinct phase regimes as polymer concentration increases — this is the heart of why Kevlar achieves such high orientation:

  • At low concentration → solution behaves as a normal isotropic fluid; viscosity increases with concentration
  • Beyond the critical concentration → viscosity drops; the solution undergoes a transition from isotropic to a partially anisotropic liquid-crystal state
  • In the liquid-crystal state, molecules are free from chain entanglements — they can align easily
  • Groups of molecules aggregate and form rod-like parallel chains, which constitute the anisotropic domains
  • Isotropic and anisotropic regions have sufficient density difference to coexist
  • These groups maintain a "safe distance" to avoid precipitation — a self-organising colloidal stability
📌
The phase diagram (LiCl concentration vs polymer concentration in DMA) shows distinct regions: Isotropic, Anisotropic + Isotropic, Anisotropic, and Anisotropic + Solid polymer. The viscosity peak in the H₂SO₄ system corresponds to the onset of anisotropy — Curve 4 (highest MW) shows the most pronounced peak.
05

Spinning Process — Dry-Jet Wet Spinning

Kevlar is manufactured by dry-jet wet spinning (also called air-gap wet spinning), exploiting the liquid-crystal nature of the dope to achieve near-perfect chain orientation without post-draw.

1

Extrusion through Spinneret

The anisotropic dope (liquid crystal domains) is pumped through a spinneret. The act of elongational flow through the spinneret holes aligns the crystal domains along the fibre axis. This is the primary orientation step.

2

Air Gap (0.5–1 cm)

The extrudate passes through a small air gap of 0.5–1 cm before entering the coagulation bath. In the air gap, the temperature is lower than that of the hot dope, causing fibre precipitation to begin. Elongation in the air gap further aligns crystal domains — this is where partial disorientation from the spinneret is recovered and improved by reorientation.

3

Coagulation Bath (0–5°C)

The fibre enters a cold coagulation bath at 0–5°C (water or dilute acid). The acid (H₂SO₄) is washed out, and the polymer precipitates / solidifies. The crystallinity of the liquid-crystal solution is transferred directly to the fibre. The pleated sheet structure characteristic of Kevlar forms here as PPTA enters the coagulation bath.

4

Washing & Drying (ASF Stage)

After coagulation, the as-spun fibre (ASF) is washed thoroughly to remove residual acid, then dried. The yarn is collected on a take-up bobbin. Spinning speed is typically 50 m/min.

5

Heat Treatment Under Tension

Properties are improved by heat treatment at 450–550°C under tension for a few seconds. This improves crystallinity and reduces fibrillar defects. The process uses tension to prevent contraction — the result is higher tenacity and modulus.

Spin Stretch Factor (SSF)

SSF = velocity of fibre leaving coagulation bath ÷ dope velocity emerging from spinneret

  • As SSF increases → tenacity and modulus increase; breaking extension decreases
  • SSF < 10 is used in practice — beyond SSF = 10, filaments break, making the process uneconomical
  • SSF is a key quality variable alongside spinning system choice (wet vs dry-jet-wet)

Advantages of Dry-Jet-Wet over Wet Spinning

  • Low coagulation temperature (0–5°C) is achievable without freezing the dope, because the air gap thermally decouples the hot spinneret from the cold bath
  • Higher concentration dope (20%) can be spun at elevated temperatures if the air gap is maintained — increases throughput and molecular alignment
  • Elongation in the air gap aligns crystal domains further, improving orientation without drawing

Orientation Comparison: Flexible vs Rod-like Polymers

Flexible Chains (e.g. Nylon)
  • In dilute solution: random coil conformation
  • Higher concentration: entangled coils — need spin-drawing to extend chains
  • Result: partially extended chains
  • Orientation factor ~ 0.6–0.7
Rod-like Chains (Kevlar)
  • Even in dilute solution: stiff rods, partially aligned
  • At critical concentration: liquid crystal state — rods self-align
  • Result: fully extended chains by spin-orientation
  • Orientation factor ~ 0.95
🔑
Key Exam Point: Kevlar is spin-oriented, NOT spin-drawn. The high orientation (~0.95) comes from the inherent stiffness of the chain and the liquid-crystal dope ordering — not from post-spinning draw ratios.

07

Secondary Structure — Hydrogen-Bonded Sheets

  • Pleated sheet configuration oriented perpendicular to the fibre axis — analogous to the β-sheet in proteins but with aromatic rings
  • Adjacent PPTA chains form hydrogen bonds between C=O and N–H groups, creating flat, planar hydrogen-bonded sheets
  • These sheets then stack together (via van der Waals / π–π interactions between benzene rings) to form a 3D crystalline domain
  • Pleats form in the core region when PPTA enters the coagulation bath — rapid coagulation "locks in" the pleating
  • The pleating of fibrils is superimposed on the fibrillar structure with a variation from linearity of ~5° and a periodicity of 500 nm
📌
Think of it as: chains bond sideways (H-bonds) to form a 2D sheet → sheets stack radially (like pages in a book arranged around the fibre axis) → this gives a cylindrical, radially symmetric fibril.
08

Tertiary Structure — Skin–Core Morphology

S
Skin (Outer Layer)
Virtually non-crystalline — rapid quench during coagulation prevents ordered packing. Forms a thin sheath around the fibre. Influences surface properties and adhesion.
C
Core (Inner Region)
High degree of crystalline order with extensive intermolecular hydrogen bonding. This is where most of the mechanical load is carried. Contains the pleated sheet fibrillar architecture.

The skin–core structure develops during spinning and post-treatment. The skin forms due to rapid solvent exchange at the surface, while the core solidifies more slowly, allowing crystallisation. This gradient in structure from surface to centre is a general feature of wet-spun and dry-jet wet-spun fibres.

09

Why Does Kevlar Show High Strength?

⚡
The combined effect of two bonding mechanisms:

1. Covalent Cross-Linkages

The covalent amide (–CO–NH–) bonds along the backbone provide the primary tensile load path. Because the chains are fully extended and perfectly aligned along the fibre axis, load is transferred along the stiffest possible backbone — no chain folding or slack.

2. Hydrogen Bonds Between Chains

The N–H···O=C hydrogen bonds in the pleated sheets resist lateral separation of chains. Because the chains are planar and aromatic, a very high density of H-bonds forms in the lateral direction — these act like transverse "stitching".

Cross-linkages (covalent backbone)  +  Hydrogen bonds (lateral sheets) ↓ HIGH STRENGTH & HIGH MODULUS

Additionally, there is no molecular mechanism for plastic deformation (no chain unfolding, no slip planes in the usual sense), so the fibre behaves elastically right up to failure — this is why it has outstanding toughness combined with high stiffness.

Dimensional Stability

  • Shrinkage in water at 100°C < 0.1% — exceptional dimensional stability in hot wet conditions
  • In dry air at 177°C < 0.1% — retains dimensions at elevated temperatures
  • No negative thermal expansion coefficient anomalies
11

Thermal Properties

  • The aromatic nature of the polymer backbone provides intrinsically very high heat resistance — the fully conjugated aromatic rings require large amounts of energy to break or rearrange
  • Kevlar fibres do not have a melting point or a conventional glass transition temperature — the crystal structure decomposes before melting
  • Flame resistant with a Limiting Oxygen Index (LOI) = 29 (air has LOI = 21; LOI > 21 means the material requires an oxygen-enriched atmosphere to sustain combustion)
  • Useful service range: –200°C to +200°C
  • Not generally recommended for prolonged use above 150°C due to progressive oxidation
  • Decomposes above 675 K (~402°C) without melting
12

Electrical & Optical Properties

Electrical

  • Electrical insulator — suitable for electrically sensitive applications
  • Dielectric constant: 4 at 10⁶ Hz
  • Stable dielectric properties across a range of frequencies

Optical (Birefringence)

  • Refractive index parallel to the fibre axis: 2.0
  • Refractive index perpendicular to fibre axis: 1.6
  • Large birefringence (Δn ≈ 0.4) confirms very high axial chain orientation
13

Chemical Properties

  • Resistant to most organic chemicals and solvents — the aromatic backbone and dense packing make it chemically inert to common solvents, oils, and fuels
  • UV radiation can degrade it — aromatic ring cleavage by high-energy photons is a known weakness; must be protected in outdoor use
  • Hypochlorite (bleach) can also cause degradation under prolonged exposure at elevated temperature
  • No embrittlement or degradation at temperatures as low as –196°C (cryogenic conditions) — exceptional low-temperature toughness
14

Environmental Behaviour (Moisture)

Kevlar contains polar amide groups (–CO–NH–) that are accessible for hydrogen bonding with water. This makes it hygroscopic:

VariantMoisture Regain (at 60% RH)
Kevlar 29 & Kevlar 49~4.0%
Kevlar 149~1.5%
⚠️
Important: Water molecules that enter the polar sites can disrupt hydrogen bonds between chains, reducing tensile strength — this is one reason Kevlar's compressive performance and wet strength are inferior to its dry tensile performance.

Advantages & Disadvantages

✔ Advantages
  • High strength-to-weight ratio — 5× stronger than steel on a weight basis
  • Excellent impact resistance — absorbs large amounts of energy before failure
  • Abrasion resistance — resists surface wear and cutting
  • Does not expand on heating — low or near-zero thermal expansion along fibre axis
  • Heat resistant — decomposes above 675 K without melting; LOI = 29
  • Chemical resistance — stable in most organic solvents and environments
  • Cryogenic toughness — no embrittlement at –196°C
✘ Disadvantages
  • Absorbs moisture — hygroscopic amide groups reduce wet strength; 4% regain for K29/K49
  • Poor compressive strength — fibrils buckle under compression; not suitable as a stand-alone structural material under compressive loads
  • Difficult to cut, drill, and machine — hardness and toughness require specialised tools
  • Degrades under UV light — requires protective coatings in outdoor applications

Exam Summary Statement: The extraordinary combination of high specific strength, high modulus, toughness, thermal stability, and chemical resistance makes Kevlar one of the most versatile high-performance fibres in engineering. Its applications span from personal protection to aerospace structures to everyday automotive components — all enabled by its unique liquid-crystal spinning route and para-aramid molecular architecture.
